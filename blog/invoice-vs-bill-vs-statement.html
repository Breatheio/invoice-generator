Invoice vs Bill vs Statement: What's the Difference?

5 min read | Updated January 2025

Invoices, bills, and statements are often used interchangeably, but they serve different purposes. Understanding these differences helps you use the right document at the right time and communicate more effectively with clients and vendors.

Quick Definitions

  • Invoice: A request for payment from seller to buyer
  • Bill: An invoice from the buyer's perspective
  • Statement: A summary of account activity over time

What Is an Invoice?

An invoice is a formal document sent by a seller to request payment from a buyer. It details what was sold, the price, and when payment is due.

When you complete work for a client and send them a document asking for $500, that's an invoice. From your perspective as the seller, you're sending an invoice.

What Is a Bill?

A bill is essentially the same document as an invoice, just from a different viewpoint. When you receive an invoice, you call it a bill.

When your client receives your $500 invoice, they see it as a bill they need to pay. Same document, different perspective.

Simple way to remember:

  • You send invoices
  • You receive bills

What Is a Statement?

A statement (or account statement) is a summary document showing all account activity over a period of time. It typically includes:

  • Opening balance
  • All invoices issued
  • All payments received
  • Any credits or adjustments
  • Closing balance (amount still owed)

Statements are useful for ongoing business relationships where multiple invoices and payments occur over time.

When to Use Each Document

Use an Invoice When:

  • You've completed work and need to request payment
  • You're billing for a specific project or order
  • You need a formal record of a sale
  • The client needs documentation for their records

Use a Statement When:

  • You have an ongoing relationship with multiple transactions
  • A client has multiple unpaid invoices
  • You want to show payment history
  • Monthly account reconciliation is needed
  • As a gentle reminder of overall balance owed

Related Terms Explained

Receipt

A receipt confirms payment has been received. Unlike an invoice (which requests payment), a receipt is issued after payment is made.

Purchase Order (PO)

A purchase order is sent by the buyer to the seller to officially request goods or services. It comes before the invoice in the process.

Quote/Estimate

A quote provides pricing before work begins. It's not a request for payment - it becomes an invoice after the work is done.

Credit Note/Memo

A credit note reduces the amount a customer owes, like a "negative invoice." Used for refunds, returns, or pricing adjustments.

Document Flow in a Typical Transaction

  1. Quote: Seller provides pricing estimate
  2. Purchase Order: Buyer formally orders
  3. Work/Delivery: Seller provides goods/services
  4. Invoice: Seller requests payment
  5. Payment: Buyer pays
  6. Receipt: Seller confirms payment received
  7. Statement: Periodic summary (if ongoing relationship)
