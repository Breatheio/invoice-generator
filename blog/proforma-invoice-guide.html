Proforma Invoice: What It Is & When to Use It

6 min read | Updated January 2025

A proforma invoice is a preliminary document that looks like an invoice but isn't a request for payment. It's commonly used in international trade, for quotes, and when clients need documentation before a sale is finalized. Here's everything you need to know.

What Is a Proforma Invoice?

"Proforma" comes from Latin meaning "as a matter of form." A proforma invoice is essentially a detailed quote or estimate formatted like an invoice. It:

  • Describes goods or services to be provided
  • Shows quantities and prices
  • Is not legally binding
  • Does not request payment
  • Can be changed before the final invoice

When to Use a Proforma Invoice

1. International Trade

Proforma invoices are essential in importing/exporting. Buyers use them to:

  • Apply for import licenses or permits
  • Arrange financing or letters of credit
  • Calculate duties and taxes in advance
  • Get approval from customs authorities

2. Detailed Quotes

When a client needs more than a simple estimate, a proforma invoice provides comprehensive pricing in a formal format.

3. Prepayment Requests

Some sellers send proforma invoices before shipping to show exactly what the buyer will receive and pay for.

4. Internal Approval

Large companies often require a proforma invoice to get budget approval before issuing a purchase order.

Proforma Invoice vs Commercial Invoice

Feature Proforma Invoice Commercial Invoice
Purpose Quote/Estimate Request payment
Legally Binding No Yes
Timing Before sale After sale/delivery
Can Be Changed Yes No (final)
For Customs Pre-approval only Required for clearance
Accounting Not recorded as revenue Recorded as revenue

What to Include on a Proforma Invoice

  • "PROFORMA INVOICE" clearly marked at the top
  • Your business name and contact information
  • Client's name and address
  • Date and reference number
  • Detailed description of goods/services
  • Quantities and unit prices
  • Total amount (including estimated taxes/duties)
  • Currency
  • Validity period (e.g., "Valid for 30 days")
  • Payment terms (for when sale is confirmed)
  • Delivery terms (Incoterms for international)
  • Estimated delivery date

Proforma Invoice Example Use Cases

Scenario 1: Exporting Goods

A US manufacturer sends a proforma invoice to a buyer in Germany. The German buyer uses it to apply for an import license and arrange a letter of credit from their bank. Once approved, the manufacturer ships the goods and sends a commercial invoice for final payment.

Scenario 2: Large Project Quote

A web development agency sends a proforma invoice to a corporate client detailing a $50,000 website project. The client's finance department uses it to get budget approval. Once approved, they issue a purchase order and the agency sends a real invoice.

Scenario 3: Deposit Collection

A furniture manufacturer sends a proforma invoice showing the full order and requesting 50% deposit. After the deposit is paid, work begins. Upon completion, a commercial invoice is sent for the remaining balance.

Important Notes

  • Always clearly label it "PROFORMA INVOICE" to avoid confusion
  • Don't record proforma invoices as revenue - they're not final sales
  • Include an expiration date since prices may change
  • Follow up with a commercial invoice when the sale is confirmed
  • Keep copies for your records

Proforma Invoice vs Quote

While similar, proforma invoices are typically more formal and detailed than quotes. Proforma invoices are often required for international trade documentation, while quotes are generally used for domestic sales discussions. A quote might say "Website development: approximately $5,000" while a proforma invoice would break down every line item with exact pricing.
